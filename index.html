Statistics

Carbon emissions are a large contributor to global warming and climate change. According to Stephanie Osmanski, the effects of climate change include melting polar ice caps, rising sea levels, the destruction of natural habitats, and increasingly severe weather (full article). Human activities contribute greatly to increased carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Examples of these activities include the use of oil, coal, and gas, as well as deforestation (full article). Currently, it is difficult to reduce CO2 emissions because of the global economy. For example, lower demand for a product causes prices to be lowered. However, low prices result in higher demand. According to Jilles van den Beukel, when coal prices decreased, the market share of coal increased significantly (full article).

A recent study showed a remarkable decline in the volume of daily emissions from carbon dioxide, worldwide, a 17% earlier April, compared to recorded levels in the same period from 2019. The study returned to government policies and prohibitions due to COVID-19 and prevent transporting between countries 17%, compared to 2019, while the decline is 26% per state. She explained that the transport sector is more declined in carbon dioxide emissions, accounting for 43% of the world's total decline, and people in their homes resulted in lack of road vehicles, while the industry and energy sector scores 43% of total. The aviation sector, although is one of the largest contamined sectors, only 3% of carbon emissions are represented in normal circumstances.

Globally, at least 2 billion people are used to drink polluted water. According to the World Health Organization, by 2025, half of the world's population will live in areas of water shortages. The data provided states that "in low and middle-income countries, 38% of health care facilities lack an improved water source, and 19% do not have improved sanitation facilities, 35% lacked water and soap to wash hands" (full article).

Climate change impacts due to increased CO2 emission have been demonstrated further by changes in the behavior of snow and ice. The United Nations reports that "by 2050, the number of people at risk of floods will increase from its current level of 1.2 billion to 1.6 billion".

The United Nations also reports an increase in global mean temperature. Beginning in 2010, the temperatures recorded have been among the warmest ever observed. Scientists predict that these temperatures will only continue to increase, with the difference being as much as 5 degrees Celsius before the end of the century (full article).

Effects of Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide is considered one of the harmful gases that affect human health, as continuous exposure to it leads to a series of side effects, and they are as follows:

  • Short-term effects. They occur when high levels of carbon dioxide are inhaled within a short time, which leads to suffocation (by replacing the air with carbon dioxide), unconsciousness, headache, vertigo and double vision, inability to concentrate, tinnitus, and convulsive seizures.
  • Long-term effects. They occur when carbon dioxide is inhaled in small amounts but regularly, which leads to changes in bone calcium or changes in the metabolism processes inside the body. Breathing carbon dioxide in large quantities can be life threatening, and if carbon dioxide gas is touches - when it is in its solid state - it may cause frostbite disease or the formation of blisters on the body.
